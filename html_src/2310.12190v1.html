Abstract

Enhancing a still image with motion offers more engaged visual experience. Traditional image animation techniques mainly focus on animating natural scenes with random dynamics, such as clouds and fluid, and thus limits their applicability to generic visual contents. To overcome this limitation, we explore the synthesis of dynamic content for open-domain images, converting them into animated videos. The key idea is to utilize the motion prior of text-to-video diffusion models by incorporating the image into the generative process as guidance. Given an image, we first project it into a text-aligned rich image embedding space using a learnable image encoding network, which facilitates the video model to digest the image content compatibly. However, some visual details still struggle to be preserved in the resulting videos. To supplement more precise image information, we further feed the full image to the diffusion model by concatenating it with the initial noises. Experimental results reveal that our proposed method produces visually convincing animated videos, exhibiting both natural motions and high fidelity to the input image. Comparative evaluation demonstrates the notable superiority of our approach over existing competitors. The source code will be released upon publication.

1 Introduction

Recent advances in generative models, specifically conditional diffusion models, have enabled us to model highly intricate and rich distributions, including those of videos conditioned on text. This capability has led to numerous previously unattainable applications, such as the text-conditioned generation of diverse and realistic videos. Given that video diffusion models effectively learn dynamic visual content, we are interested in exploring the possibility of leveraging video diffusion priors to animate arbitrary still images. It has the potential to overcome the domain limitations of existing traditional image animation techniques and significantly advance the field.

To this end, we propose an open-domain image animation approach, called DynamiCrafter. The key idea is to harness the motion prior of text-to-video diffusion models by integrating the image into the generative process as guidance. This is a non-trivial task because text-to-video models are trained using text conditions that are more semantic than concrete visual signals like images. To address this challenge, we propose a dual-stream conditional image injection paradigm, comprising text-aligned image embedding projection and visual detail modulation. Given an image, we first project it into the text-aligned rich image embedding space using a learnable image encoding network, allowing the video model to interpret the image content compatibly. However, the extracted image embedding can only make the generated videos closely resemble the input image but may still struggle to preserve all visual details. To provide more precise image information, we further feed the full image to the diffusion model by concatenating it with the initial noises. This coarse-to-fine injection strategy ensures that the generated video shares the same appearance as the input image while presenting vivid dynamic content.

By comparing with existing competitors, our proposed method achieves notable superiority both in video quality and visual fidelity to the conditional image. Thanks to the decent visual fidelity, our proposed DynamiCrafter enables practical applications, such as storytelling video generation. For example, we can first generate high-quality image through text-to-image models (Stable Diffusion [53]) and their control tools (like T2I adapter [46] or ControlNet [84]) for each story plot, and then animate them through our proposed method, which finally composites a coherent storytelling video.

2 Related Work

2.1 Image Animation

Image animation, a well-established research area, involves generating a video from one or more input still images. Early physical simulation-based approaches [32, 9] focus on simulating the motion of specific objects with known or computed identities, resulting in low generalizability due to the independent modeling of each object category. To produce more realistic motions, numerous reference-based methods [51, 49, 59, 8, 33, 58, 60, 71] transfer motion or appearance information from reference signals, such as videos, to the synthesis process. Although these methods demonstrate greater temporal coherence, the need for additional guidance signals limits their practical application. Additionally, a line of GAN-based works [56, 23, 34] successfully generate temporally varying frames by perturbing initial latents or performing random walk in the latent vector space. However, the motion in their animation is not plausible since the animation frames are just a visualization of the possible appearance space without temporal awareness. Recently, (learned) motion prior-based methods [14, 28, 44, 47, 73, 87, 74, 31] animate input still images through explicit or implicit image-based rendering with estimated motion field or geometry priors. Similarly, video prediction approaches [83, 76, 1, 38, 77, 67, 30, 29, 16] predict future video frames starting from single still images by modeling spatio-temporal priors in video data using deep neural networks.

Although existing approaches has achieved impressive performance, they primarily focus on animating motions in curated domains, particularly stochastic [14, 32, 5, 78, 49, 9, 36, 12] and oscillation [40] motion. Furthermore, the animated objects are limited to specific categories, e.g., fluid [28, 49, 28, 43], natural scenes [76, 40, 8, 32, 56], human hair [74], portraits [71, 70, 18], and bodies [71, 73, 33, 60, 6, 4]. In contrast to these methods, our work proposes a general framework for animating open-domain images with a wide range of content and styles by generating natural-looking dynamics, which is extremely challenging due to the overwhelming complexity and vast diversity.

2.2 Video Diffusion Models (VDMs)

Diffusion models (DMs) [62, 25, 65] have recently shown unprecedented generative semantic and compositional power in text-to-image (T2I) generation [53, 48, 55, 19, 54, 3, 22, 86, 50]. To replicate the success of T2I DMs to video generation, Video Diffusion Model [27] models low-resolution videos with DMs using a space-time factorized U-Net in pixel space and is trained jointly on image and video data. To generate high-definition videos, Imagen-Video [26] presents effective cascaded DMs with 𝐯𝐯\mathbf{v}bold_v-prediction parameterization method. To further reduce the training costs, subsequent studies [68, 21, 7, 88, 72] are engaged in transferring T2I knowledge to text-to-video generation (T2V) [61, 41] and learning DMs in latent space. Most recently, Zhang et al. [82] highlight the issue of heavy computational cost in pixel-based VDM [17] and poor text-video alignment in latent-based VDM, proposing a hybrid-pixel-latent VDM framework to address these issues.

Although these models can generate high-quality videos, they only accept text prompts as semantic guidance, which can be verbose and may not accurately reflect users’ intentions. Similar to adding controls in T2I models [39, 84, 46, 79, 57], introducing control conditions in T2V DMs has increasingly attracted researchers’ attention. Gen-1 [15] and Make-Your-Video [75] integrate structure control into VDMs by concatenating frame-wise depth map with input noise sequences for video editing, while other control conditions, including pose [42, 85] and canny edge [85, 35] are also investigated. However, visual conditions in VDMs, such as RGB images, remain underexplored. Most recently, image condition is examined in Seer [20] and VideoComposer [69] for text-image-to-video synthesis, by keeping first-frame latent clean and concatenating image embedding with noise in channel dimension, respectively. Nevertheless, they either focus on the curated domain, i.e., indoor objects [20], or fail to generate temporally coherent frames and realistic motions [69] due to insufficient semantic understanding of the input image. Although DragNUWA [80] further introduce trajectory control into image-to-video generation, which can only mitigate the unrealistic-motion issue to some extent. Moreover, some recent close-sourced text-to-video diffusion models [61, 45, 37] or auto-regressive models [66, 81] successfully demonstrate their extension applicability to image-to-video synthesis. However, their results rarely adhere to the input image condition and suffers from the unrealistic temporal variation issue. While we build our model upon text-conditioned VDMs to leverage their rich dynamic prior for animating open-domain images, incorporating tailored designs for better conformity and semantic understanding of the input image.

3 Method

Given a still image, we aim to animate it as a short video clip that inherits all the visual contents from the image while exhibiting natural dynamics. Technically, we approach this challenge by framing it as an image-conditioned video generation task, and propose to address it by leveraging the generative priors of pre-trained video diffusion models. In the following sections, we will first provide a brief overview of video diffusion models, and then elaborate how to generate a high-fidelity video from a given image.

3.2 Image Dynamics from Video Diffusion Priors

An open-domain text-to-video generation model is presumed to encompass various video clips within its distribution. To animate a specific image, the model is required to calculate the marginal distribution based on the given image. Intuitively, the objective is to sample a video within the modeling space that has the provided image as one of its frames. For text-to-video diffusion models, the conditional text embedding space plays the primary role in determining the semantic contents of the final output videos, while the initial noise space only affects the concrete visual elements. Regarding this observation, we propose a dual-stream conditional image injection paradigm, including text-aligned image embedding projection and visual detail modulation.

Text-Aligned Rich Image Embedding.

Visual Detail Modulation.

By conditioning on the text-aligned rich image embedding, the video model can generate videos that closely resemble the input image. However, some minor visual inconsistencies may still be observed. This could be attributed to the pretrained CLIP image encoder’s inability to fully preserve the input image information, as it is trained using multimodal contrastive learning over image-text pairs. Nevertheless, utilizing the pretrained CLIP image encoder enables the text-to-video model to effectively incorporate image features in a compatible manner, reducing the risk of semantic distortion in the generated videos. To further enhance the accuracy of visual details from the input image, we employ a coarse-to-fine visual injection strategy. Initially, we learn a text-aligned rich image embedding, and then modulate the visual details by feeding the complete conditional image into the denoising U-Net, concatenating it with per-frame initial noises. This approach allows the model to utilize the full image as a subtle reference for refining the visual elements.

4 Experiment

4.1 Implementation Details

Our development is based on the open-sourced text-conditioned VideoLDM VideoCrafter [11] and T2I LDM Stable-Diffusion-v2.1 (SD) [54], implemented with PyTorch and the public pre-trained weights. We firstly introduce the CLIP image encoder and projection network 𝒫𝒫\mathcal{P}caligraphic_P to SD, and then train 𝒫𝒫\mathcal{P}caligraphic_P and the newly injected image cross-attention layers only, with 1000K steps on the learning rate 1×10−41superscript1041\times 10^{-4}1 × 10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT - 4 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT and valid mini-batch size 64. Then we replace SD with VideoLDM and further fine-tune the whole model with additional 100K steps on the learning rate 5×10−55superscript1055\times 10^{-5}5 × 10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT - 5 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT and valid mini-batch size 64. At inference, we adopt DDIM sampler [64] with classifier-free guidance [24] in all experiments. Our DynamiCrafter was trained on WebVid-10M [2] dataset, which consists of around 10 million caotion-video pairs with a total of 52K video hours. During training, we sample 16 frames with a random frame stride s𝑠sitalic_s, uniformly sampled from {1,…,6}1…6\{1,\ldots,6\}{ 1 , … , 6 }, and resolutions of 256×256256256256\times 256256 × 256 and 320×512320512320\times 512320 × 512 from input videos.

4.2 Comparative Evaluation

Since open-domain image-to-video synthesis is a nascent area of computer vision and graphics, we find a limited number of publicly available research works to compare against. We qualitatively evaluate our method against VideoComposer [69], a recent approach for compositional video generation, which supports text-image-to-video synthesis, and I2VGen-XL [10], an open-sourced image-to-video generation project. The visual comparison results can be found in Figure 3. Across all compared methods, videos generated by our approach are temporally coherent and adhere to the input image condition. In contrast, VideoComposer struggles to produce consistent video frames, as subsequent frames tend to deviate from the initial frame due to a suboptimal semantic understanding of the input image; I2VGen-XL can only generate videos that semantically resemble the input images, but fails to preserve intricate local visual details. Furthermore, as demonstrated by the anime, cartoon, and campfire examples in Figure 3, the overall quality and aesthetic appeal of the videos produced by I2VGen-XL are notably inferior to those produced by our DynamiCrafter. Additionally, we show more image animation results with diverse content and styles at a higher resolution (320×512320512320\times 512320 × 512) in Figure 4.

5 Conclusion

In this study, we present an effective approach to synthesize dynamic content from open-domain images by leveraging pre-trained text-to-video diffusion models and incorporating the image as guidance. We introduce a dual-stream conditional information injection strategy to guide the generation process in both semantic-level and pixel-level, which ensures that the generated video preserves the appearance of the input image while exhibiting vivid dynamics. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness and superiority of our approach compared to existing competitors.
