Accelerating Text-to-Video Generation with Calibrated Sparse Attention

Shai Yehezkel Apple Tel Aviv University    Shahar Yadin Apple Tel Aviv University    Noam Elata Apple Tel Aviv University    Yaron Ostrovsky-Berman Apple Tel Aviv University    Bahjat Kawar Apple Tel Aviv University
Abstract

Recent diffusion models enable high-quality video generation, but suffer from slow runtimes. The large transformer-based backbones used in these models are bottlenecked by spatiotemporal attention. In this paper, we identify that a significant fraction of token-to-token connections consistently yield negligible scores across various inputs, and their patterns often repeat across queries. Thus, the attention computation in these cases can be skipped with little to no effect on the result. This observation continues to hold for connections among local token blocks. Motivated by this, we introduce CalibAtt, a training-free method that accelerates video generation via calibrated sparse attention. CalibAtt performs an offline calibration pass that identifies block-level sparsity and repetition patterns that are stable across inputs, and compiles these patterns into optimized attention operations for each layer, head, and diffusion timestep. At inference time, we compute the selected input-dependent connections densely, and skip the unselected ones in a hardware-efficient manner. Extensive experiments on Wan 2.1 14B, Mochi 1, and few-step distilled models at various resolutions show that CalibAtt achieves up to 1.58×1.58\times end-to-end speedup, outperforming existing training-free methods while maintaining video generation quality and text-video alignment.

1 Introduction

Transformers [vaswani2017attention] have been established as a foundational architecture, revolutionizing many domains such as natural language processing [brown2020language, grattafiori2024llama], computer vision [dosovitskiy2021an, oquab2024dinov2], and generative models such as image and video diffusion models [ho2020denoisingdiffusionprobabilisticmodels, peebles2023scalable, esser2024scaling, wan2025]. Their state-of-the-art performance, however, comes at a significant inference-time computational cost. The self-attention mechanism, central to the transformer architecture, has a quadratic complexity with respect to sequence length, making it particularly challenging for longer sequences inherent in many applications. In this work, we propose training-free optimizations for self-attention, focusing on the video generation application for several reasons: (i) they represent a powerful and increasingly useful tool for content generation; (ii) they inherently require long sequences to support higher resolutions and larger frame counts; and (iii) video data often contains high levels of spatiotemporal redundancy, making it an ideal candidate for compute savings.

To mitigate the computational demands of attention, various innovations have been proposed [zhang2026survey]. Most notably, FlashAttention [dao2022flashattention, dao2023flashattention2fasterattentionbetter, shah2024flashattention3] significantly reduces the memory and runtime of the attention operation. However, it does not change the number of multiplication operations. Many papers [zhang2026survey] explore reducing the amount of computation required for attention, often adhering to FlashAttention-friendly constraints leading to improved runtime gains. Despite their advancements, these methods often require fine-tuning, or offer limited acceleration by disabling their methods on certain layers or diffusion timesteps.

In this work, we propose CalibAtt, a novel training-free method for accelerating attention in video diffusion transformers that automatically calibrates to any model, works in tandem with FlashAttention3 [shah2024flashattention3], and offers state-of-the-art runtime savings. We begin by inspecting attention maps in video models and making observations about their typical behaviors at the token level and the block level. We identify sparse and repetitive attention patterns, many of which are well-replicated across various text prompts and latent initial noise inputs. However, these patterns vary significantly in different transformer layers, attention heads, and in some cases, diffusion timesteps. Based on these observations, we devise an algorithm that automatically identify data-independent patterns at the FlashAttention block level, and calibrate either an attention mask or mark repeating queries for each layer, head, and timestep combination in a model.

We conduct extensive experiments on several prominent open-source video diffusion models: Wan 2.1 [wan2025], Mochi 1 [genmo2024mochi], and LightX2V [lightx2v], a few-step distilled model. We apply our method on different output resolutions and number of diffusion steps. In all scenarios, CalibAtt consistently maintains the generation quality of the slower dense attention baseline, while achieving state-of-the-art attention sparsity and end-to-end runtime among training-free acceleration methods. Furthermore, because of the calibration’s minimal reliance on heuristics, CalibAtt’s performance persists across models with negligible sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning, and does not require arbitrary exclusion of specific layers or timesteps. Finally, we conduct hyperparameter ablations that affect CalibAtt’s inference runtime, memory footprint, and the one-time cost of the calibration step. Notably, the results show that CalibAtt produces impressively sparse masks even at low calibration budgets.

2 Preliminaries and Related Work

Attention [vaswani2017attention] has become a fundamental building block in modern neural architectures. Formally, the attention operation is defined over queries 𝐐∈ℝNq×d{\mathbf{Q}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{q}\times d}, keys 𝐊∈ℝNk​v×d{\mathbf{K}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{kv}\times d}, and values 𝐕∈ℝNk​v×d{\mathbf{V}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{kv}\times d}, where dd is the feature dimension, and NqN_{q}, Nk​vN_{kv} are the query and key-value sequence lengths, respectively. The attention output 𝐀∈ℝNq×d{\mathbf{A}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{q}\times d} is computed as

𝐏=softmax​(𝐐𝐊⊤d),{\mathbf{P}}=\text{softmax}\left(\frac{{\mathbf{Q}}{\mathbf{K}}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}}\right), (1) 𝐀=𝐏𝐕.{\mathbf{A}}={\mathbf{P}}{\mathbf{V}}. (2) (2)

Typically, multi-headed attention is used, repeating this operation for hh heads, each with its own set of keys, queries, and values. As sequence lengths grow, materializing the dense attention matrix 𝐏∈ℝNq×Nk​v{\mathbf{P}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{q}\times N_{kv}} quickly becomes prohibitive due to memory constraints, and when Nq,Nk​v≫dN_{q},N_{kv}\gg d, the cost of processing this matrix also comes to dominate runtime. FlashAttention [dao2022flashattention] (FA) addresses this by tiling the computation into blocks, ensuring that only a small portion of the attention matrix resides in fast memory at any given time. This approach avoids the quadratic memory consumption while also providing significant runtime improvements through better hardware utilization. Concretely, FA partitions the query and key/value sequences into contiguous blocks of size BqB_{q} and Bk​vB_{kv}, respectively. Let

ℐr={i∣r​Bq≤i<(r+1)​Bq},𝒥c={j∣c​Bk​v≤j<(c+1)​Bk​v},\mathcal{I}_{r}=\{\,i\mid rB_{q}\leq i<(r+1)B_{q}\,\},\qquad\mathcal{J}_{c}=\{\,j\mid cB_{kv}\leq j<(c+1)B_{kv}\,\}, (3)

denote the index sets of the rr-th query block and cc-th key/value block. We denote the corresponding query, key, and value blocks by 𝐐r=𝐐​[ℐr,:]{{\mathbf{Q}}_{r}={\mathbf{Q}}[\mathcal{I}_{r},:]}, 𝐊c=𝐊​[𝒥c,:]{{\mathbf{K}}_{c}={\mathbf{K}}[\mathcal{J}_{c},:]}, and 𝐕c=𝐕​[𝒥c,:]{{\mathbf{V}}_{c}={\mathbf{V}}[\mathcal{J}_{c},:]}. The block-wise attention computation is then given by

𝐀~r,c=softmax​(𝐐r​𝐊c⊤d−mr)​𝐕c,\tilde{{\mathbf{A}}}_{r,c}=\text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{{\mathbf{Q}}_{r}{\mathbf{K}}_{c}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}}-m_{r}\right){\mathbf{V}}_{c}, (4)

where mrm_{r} is a running maximum used for numerical stability. The final output for each query block is obtained by rescaling and accumulating contributions across all key-value blocks using online softmax [milakov2018online]. For full derivations, see [dao2022flashattention].

While FlashAttention improves the runtime of attention computation, the total number of multiplication operations remains unchanged. To reduce computational cost, many recent methods propose constraining attention to specific structures or patterns that reflect assumptions about token-to-token correlations [Beltagy2020Longformer, liu2021swin, hassani2023neighborhood, huang2019ccnet, wang2020axial, yang2021focal, zhu2021deformable]. Due to the restrictive nature of these attention patterns, they are typically chosen during architectural design and can rarely be applied to models trained with dense (standard) attention without fine-tuning.

A complementary line of research exploits the inherent sparsity of typical attention maps, enabling acceleration without architectural modifications. These methods observe that when tokens are mismatched, the corresponding elements of 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} are nearly zero, and thus their computation in Eq.˜2 can be skipped entirely, eliminating redundant operations. However, identifying which elements or blocks can be safely skipped a priori is non-trivial, and a variety of approaches have been proposed to address this challenge. For a detailed survey, see [zhang2026survey].

Several approaches [zhang2025sla, wu2025vmoba, liu2025fpsattention, zhang2025vsa] achieve sparsity by finetuning the model to adapt its attention layers. While these methods can reach high sparsity levels by explicitly optimizing models to do so, they require access to significant computational resources and high-quality training data which may not be available.

Alternatively, training-free sparsity methods apply sparse attention mechanisms to pre-trained models without additional training. Some works [zhang2025fast, liradial] pre-compute attention masks based on positional and structural priors and apply them on 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} at inference time with minimal runtime overhead. For instance, Radial Attention [liradial] employs a static sparse attention mask, where each token attends to a window of spatially nearby tokens, with the window size shrinking exponentially with temporal distance. In contrast, methods with online decisions [xi2025sparse, zhang2025spargeattn, shmilovich2025liteattention, xu2025xattentionblocksparseattention, xia2025trainingfreeadaptivesparseattention, ohayon2025blocksparseflashattention] identify sparsity patterns during inference by profiling attention characteristics, enabling input-adaptive sparsity at the cost of inference-time overhead. For instance, SpargeAttention [zhang2025spargeattn] estimates block importance online by computing attention on compressed queries and keys, skipping blocks below a threshold, and introduces skip lists to specify which block intervals to compute. LiteAttention [shmilovich2025liteattention] builds upon SpargeAttention, adapting it into a FlashAttention3-based kernel and reusing skip lists across timesteps. Sparse VideoGen 2 [yang2025sparse] clusters tokens, computes attention among cluster centroids, and computes token-level attention only within high-scoring cluster interactions.

In our work, we focus on the idea of using pre-computed masks to accelerate attention without inference-time overhead. Instead of assuming fixed decay patterns, we compute masks through model-dependent calibration. Our calibration algorithm adapts to the inherent attention patterns of each model, diffusion timestep, layer, and attention head, while remaining largely agnostic to the specific calibration data, thereby achieving higher sparsity than methods relying on pre-defined masks.

3 Method

3.1 Initial Inspection and Observations

We begin by analyzing the typical behavior of attention layers in text-to-video diffusion models for various input prompts and seeds. Specifically, we focus on the use case of generating 480​p480p videos with Wan 2.1 14B [wan2025], and we find that the observations generalize across other settings. We make four key observations.

Observation 1: Many attention maps are sparse.

We examine the N×NN\times N post-softmax attention matrix 𝐏{\mathbf{P}}. In the vast majority of cases, we notice that it focuses on a small number of token-to-token interactions (see Fig.˜2). The rest of the tokens receive a negligible amount of attention, rendering their inclusion in the output value summation redundant. Furthermore, we observe that the sparsity remains very apparent, even when considering B×BB\times B blocks in the attention matrix 𝐏{\mathbf{P}}, where each block represents the query-wise average of the post-softmax attention, summed over keys. This observation highlights attention as a prime candidate for compute savings at inference time, especially since block-wise sparsity is compatible with hardware-friendly efficient attention kernels [dao2022flashattention, shah2024flashattention3]. Similar observations have been made in previous work [xi2025sparse, liradial, zhang2025vsa].

Observation 2: Attention patterns do not replicate across heads, layers, and timesteps.

As can be seen in Fig.˜2, the attention patterns are inconsistent for different heads in the same layer, different layers in the same timestep, as well as for different timesteps in the same layer and head. In a successful model training run, different attention layers and heads usually learn to focus on different semantic concepts [voita2019analyzing]. Hence, when proposing a sparsity-based acceleration technique, we argue it is preferable to fit a specific attention mask for each combination of layer, head, timestep, rather than use a fixed mask for all of them [liradial]. That being said, we note that timesteps are a special case. Later timesteps, corresponding to lower noise levels, often exhibit highly similar attention patterns for a given layer and head, a phenomenon also observed in prior work [shmilovich2025liteattention]. We analyze this behavior in Sec.˜0.A.3.

Observation 3: Attention patterns persist across inputs.

We observe that the sparsity patterns of attention maps are persistent for various input prompts and initial diffusion noise vectors. Despite the different semantic contents generated with different prompts, attention connections tend to focus on the same spatio-temporal areas in most cases, as shown in Fig.˜3(a). This observation motivates the one-time offline calibration of data-independent sparse attention masks, to be used online and alleviate the computational cost of video generation inference with no significant overhead. We explore this in Sec.˜3.2.

Observation 4: Attention patterns repeat across spatial rows.

We further observe that some attention maps exhibit a repetition structure within frame to frame blocks. In such cases, attention scores in 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} corresponding to queries of spatial rows within the same frame exhibit highly similar patterns. For these maps, computing attention for a single representative row per frame may suffice [willettedelta], with remaining rows recoverable via repeating the attention output. We visualize an example of this behavior in Fig.˜4. Interestingly, we find that attention maps exhibiting this repetition structure tend to have low block sparsity, suggesting that spatial replication and block sparsification are complementary acceleration strategies. We elaborate on this in Sec.˜3.3.

3.2 Attention Mask Calibration

We now turn to describing CalibAtt. Given a model and an inference configuration, our goal is to produce a binary block mask 𝐌(t,l,h){\mathbf{M}}^{(t,l,h)} for each diffusion timestep tt, transformer layer ll, and attention head hh, whose entries indicate whether each block is computed (11) or skipped (0).

Per-prompt energy-based block selection.

At a fixed (t,l,h)(t,l,h), we consider the post-softmax attention map 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} (queries ×\times keys) of size N×NN\times N defined in Eq.˜1. Motivated by Observation 1, we adopt an energy viewpoint: for each query, attention mass is often concentrated on a small subset of keys, while the remaining entries contribute negligibly to the output.

To identify which parts of 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} can be skipped efficiently in hardware, we operate at block granularity. We partition 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} into contiguous blocks of size B×BB\times B, assuming equal block sizes for queries and keys/values for simplicity, and ablate on different configurations in Sec.˜0.B.3. This yields NB=N/BN_{B}=N/B blocks per dimension. For each query block-row rr and key block-column cc, we define the block energy as the sum of attention scores over keys in cc, averaged over queries in rr:

𝐄r,c=1B​∑i∈ℐr∑j∈𝒥c𝐏i​j,{\mathbf{E}}_{r,c}\;=\;\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}_{r}}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{J}_{c}}{\mathbf{P}}_{ij}, (5)

where ℐr\mathcal{I}_{r} and 𝒥c\mathcal{J}_{c} are the query and key/value index sets defined in Eq. 3. Intuitively, 𝐄r,c{\mathbf{E}}_{r,c} measures how much attention mass the queries in block-row rr allocate to keys in block-column cc, averaged across the BB queries. Averaging this way ensures that a block-row energy 𝐄r{\mathbf{E}}_{r} sums up to 11.

For each query block-row rr, our goal is to keep the smallest number of key blocks whose cumulative energy reaches a prescribed threshold:

min𝒮r⊆{1,…,NB}⁡|𝒮r|s.t.∑c∈𝒮r𝐄r,c≥ϵ.\min_{\mathcal{S}_{r}\subseteq\{1,\dots,N_{B}\}}|\mathcal{S}_{r}|\quad\text{s.t.}\quad\sum_{c\in\mathcal{S}_{r}}{\mathbf{E}}_{r,c}\;\geq\;\epsilon. (6)

We define the energy threshold to be timestep-dependent via ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t). This follows prior observations that applying aggressive sparsification too early in the denoising process can lead to noticeably larger degradation in output quality [liradial, yang2025sparse]. In practice, we use an exponential schedule over timesteps t∈{0,…,T−1}t\in\{0,\dots,T-1\}, where t=0t=0 denotes the highest-noise step:

ϵ​(t)=A+(C−A)​exp⁡(−k​t/T),\epsilon(t)\;=\;A+(C-A)\exp\left(-kt/T\right), (7)

where TT is the total number of diffusion steps, and AA, CC, and kk are schedule hyperparameters. In practice, ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t) remains high throughout, ranging from 0.990.99 to 0.840.84 in Wan [wan2025]. We provide details on the schedule hyperparameter search in the supplementary material Sec.˜0.A.1.

Given ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t) and a text prompt pp, we compute 𝒮r\mathcal{S}_{r} by sorting {𝐄r,c}c\{{\mathbf{E}}_{r,c}\}_{c} in descending order and selecting the smallest prefix whose cumulative energy reaches ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t), marking the selected blocks as kept and the remainder as skipped. This algorithm optimally solves Eq.˜6 and yields a per-prompt binary block mask 𝐌p(t,l,h)∈{0,1}NB×NB{\mathbf{M}}_{p}^{(t,l,h)}\in\{0,1\}^{N_{B}\times N_{B}}, where [𝐌p(t,l,h)]r,c=1\big[{\mathbf{M}}_{p}^{(t,l,h)}\big]_{r,c}\!=\!1 indicates that block (r,c)(r,c) is computed and 0 indicates it is skipped. This procedure is illustrated in Fig.˜5(a) (top).

Cross-prompt mask aggregation.

Motivated by Observation 3, we aim to identify block connections that can be safely skipped in a data-independent manner. While the per-prompt energy selection described above yields a binary mask for a single input, our goal is to compile a single calibrated mask per (t,l,h)(t,l,h) that is robust across different prompts and noise initializations.

Concretely, for each (t,l,h)(t,l,h) we run the energy-based selection procedure on a set of calibration prompts 𝒟\mathcal{D}, producing prompt-specific masks 𝐌p(t,l,h){\mathbf{M}}_{p}^{(t,l,h)} for all p∈𝒟p\in\mathcal{D}. We aggregate these masks by averaging them elementwise:

𝐌¯(t,l,h)=1|𝒟|​∑p∈𝒟𝐌p(t,l,h).\bar{{\mathbf{M}}}^{(t,l,h)}\;=\;\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|}\sum_{p\in\mathcal{D}}{\mathbf{M}}_{p}^{(t,l,h)}. (8)

where each entry [𝐌¯(t,l,h)]r,c∈[0,1]\big[\bar{{\mathbf{M}}}^{(t,l,h)}\big]_{r,c}\in[0,1] indicates how frequently block (r,c)(r,c) was computed across prompts. In all experiments, we use |𝒟|=64|\mathcal{D}|=64 calibration prompts and ablate its effect in Fig.˜7.

As shown in Fig.˜3(b), the distribution is bimodal: many blocks are either almost always computed or almost always skipped, suggesting that many connections can be pruned reliably. We then obtain the final calibrated mask 𝐌(t,l,h){\mathbf{M}}^{(t,l,h)} by thresholding each mask block [𝐌¯(t,l,h)]r,c\big[\bar{{\mathbf{M}}}^{(t,l,h)}\big]_{r,c} with an agreement threshold ρ\rho:

[𝐌(t,l,h)]r,c={1,[𝐌¯(t,l,h)]r,c≥ρ0,[𝐌¯(t,l,h)]r,c<ρ,\big[{\mathbf{M}}^{(t,l,h)}\big]_{r,c}\;=\;\begin{cases}1,&\big[\bar{{\mathbf{M}}}^{(t,l,h)}\big]_{r,c}\geq\rho\\[2.0pt] 0,&\big[\bar{{\mathbf{M}}}^{(t,l,h)}\big]_{r,c}<\rho\end{cases}, (9)

where repeating this for all (t,l,h)(t,l,h) yields a dictionary of calibrated masks {𝐌(t,l,h)}\{{\mathbf{M}}^{(t,l,h)}\}, as illustrated in Fig.˜5(a) (center). At inference time, we retrieve the appropriate mask by indexing this dictionary with the current diffusion step, layer, and head, as illustrated in Fig.˜5(b) (top). The threshold ρ\rho determines how to handle the non-unanimous blocks, controlling the tradeoff between increased sparsity as ρ\rho increases, at the cost of decreased fidelity to the original model behavior since we skip more blocks. This threshold is largely independent of the underlying algorithm used in per-prompt mask calibration. In all experiments, we set ρ=0.5\rho=0.5, and ablate its effect on quality in Sec. 4.2.

3.3 Spatial Repetition Detection

Motivated by Observation 4, we identify attention maps that exhibit repetitive patterns across spatial query positions within each frame, enabling additional computational savings beyond block skipping. We examine the attention patterns for tokens within a spatial row ii of a video frame ff. Due to row-major ordering, these tokens have consecutive indices

ℐ(f,i)={l|f​H​W+i​W≤l<f​H​W+(i+1)​W},\mathcal{I}^{(f,i)}=\left\{l|fHW+iW\leq l<fHW+\left(i+1\right)W\right\}, (10)

where WW is the number of tokens in a row, and HH is the number of rows in a frame. Their corresponding post-softmax attention values are denoted as 𝐏(f,i)=flatten​(𝐏​[ℐ(f,i),:]){{\mathbf{P}}^{(f,i)}=\mathrm{flatten}\left({\mathbf{P}}[\mathcal{I}^{(f,i)},:]\right)}, referring to 𝐏{\mathbf{P}} from Eq.˜1.

We observe that for some attention maps, attention patterns are highly similar across spatial rows within a frame, i.e., 𝐏(f,i)≈𝐏(f,j){\mathbf{P}}^{(f,i)}\approx{\mathbf{P}}^{(f,j)} for all i,j∈{1,…,H}i,j\in\{1,\dots,H\}. Thus, in these maps, we can select kk equispaced anchor spatial rows per frame, compute their attention against all keys and values, and broadcast each result to its nearest spatial rows within that frame. This reduces the number of computed query tokens per frame from H​WHW to k​WkW, inducing a sparsity of 1−k/H1-k/H.

To identify which attention maps exhibit this structure, we compute a spatial similarity score s(t,l,h)s^{(t,l,h)} at each (t,l,h)(t,l,h) as follows: We compute the cosine similarity between each 𝐏(f,i){\mathbf{P}}^{(f,i)} and its nearest anchor row, then average over ff, ii, and the input prompts to obtain s(t,l,h)s^{(t,l,h)}. When s(t,l,h)s^{(t,l,h)} exceeds a threshold γ\gamma, we mark the corresponding (t,l,h)(t,l,h) to be computed as spatially repetitive during inference (Fig.˜5(a) bottom). We ablate on the number of anchor rows kk and the similarity threshold γ\gamma in Sec.˜0.B.1, and choose k=5k=5, γ=0.87\gamma=0.87 in all experiments.

We find that attention maps with high spatial similarity tend to have lower block sparsity, and vice versa, as shown in Fig.˜6(a). Hence, these two acceleration strategies are complementary. Additionally, maps exhibiting high spatial similarity do so consistently across different input prompts, as shown in Fig.˜6(b), allowing us to reliably identify repetitive maps using a few prompts.

3.4 Efficient Implementation

Calibration stage.

For block-level attention energies 𝐄r,c{\mathbf{E}}_{r,c} (Eq. 5), we implemented a custom CUDA kernel that operates at block granularity and accumulates the required statistics without materializing the full attention matrix 𝐏{\mathbf{P}}. Spatial similarity scores s(t,l,h)s^{(t,l,h)} (Sec.˜3.3) are computed separately using an optimized batched PyTorch implementation.

Inference stage.

Several inference frameworks support block-sparse attention computation [dong2024flexattentionprogrammingmodel, ye2025flashinferefficientcustomizableattention]. We opt to build a separate custom CUDA kernel for CalibAtt, implementing optimizations specific to pre-computed masks that vary per timestep, layer, and head. Our implementation is based on FlashAttention3 [shah2024flashattention3] and block-sparse kernels proposed in prior work [zhang2025spargeattn, shmilovich2025liteattention]. The kernel operates on read-only skip lists, precomputed from the calibrated attention masks as such: For each calibrated binary block mask 𝐌(t,l,h){\mathbf{M}}^{(t,l,h)} and each query block-row rr, we encode the contiguous ranges of key block-columns cc for which the corresponding attention blocks should be computed. All skip lists are precomputed once during calibration and preloaded onto the GPU before inference. During generation, the appropriate skip list is selected based on (t,l,h)(t,l,h) and passed to the attention kernel at launch time (Fig.˜5(b) top). For attention heads flagged as spatially repetitive, we instead use standard FlashAttention3 with a reduced query set containing only the anchor spatial rows, and broadcast the result to all spatial rows within each frame (Fig.˜5(b) bottom). We provide more details and optimizations in Sec.˜0.A.2.

4 Experiments

4.1 Setup

Models.

We evaluate CalibAtt on two different model architectures: (i) Wan2.1 14B [wan2025], a 14B-parameter model, generating 55-second videos (8181 frames) in T=50T=50 timesteps in two resolutions: 480​p480p and 720​p720p, and (ii) Mochi 1 [genmo2024mochi], a 10B-parameter model which generates 480​p480p videos of 8585 frames in T=64T=64 timesteps. We also evaluate on LightX2V [lightx2v], a 44-step distilled variant of Wan2.1 14B, using the same resolutions and frame counts.

Baselines.

We use FlashAttention3 (FA3) [shah2024flashattention3] as a reference dense attention baseline. We additionally compare against training-free attention acceleration methods: RadialAttention [liradial], SparseVideoGen2 (SVG2) [yang2025sparse], and SpargeAttention [zhang2025spargeattn]. All methods are evaluated using identical pretrained checkpoints and model inference settings, and they are all implemented with a FlashAttention3 backend or equivalent. For more details, please refer to Sec. 0.A.4.

Metrics.

We evaluate generation quality as well as text–video alignment using VBench [huang2023vbench] on its official evaluation prompts and metric suite, reporting Semantic, Quality, and Total scores. We focus on generation quality and prompt adherence rather than per-seed reconstruction metrics (e.g., PSNR), as our goal is to preserve the overall quality level of generated videos rather than to exactly replicate the dense baseline outputs. To quantify efficiency, we report Sparsity, defined as the fraction of skipped query-key interactions out of all spatiotemporal token pairs in self-attention, averaged over all timesteps, layers, and attention heads. Additionally, we report end-to-end Latency, defined as the wall-clock time of the diffusion process to generate a single video, averaged over all evaluation prompts, and Speedup relative to dense FA3.

Implementation details.

All evaluations are performed on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU. A single calibration trial consists of estimating sparse attention masks using a calibration set of |𝒟|=64|\mathcal{D}|=64 prompts sampled from MovieGenBench [polyak2025moviegencastmedia] with an agreement threshold of ρ=0.5\rho=0.5. All masks are defined at FlashAttention block granularity of 128×128128\times 128. We choose energy threshold schedule ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t) hyperparameters that perform reasonably well without model-specific tuning. Specifically, in low-step regimes, we set AA, CC, kk to constants, and in high-step regimes, we set CC, kk to constants and AA to be linearly dependent on the sequence length. We provide more details in Sec.˜0.A.1.

Results.

We summarize our results on high-timestep regimes in Tab.˜1. CalibAtt achieves the best attention sparsity and latency, while maintaining VBench performance comparable to dense FA3, across different models and different resolutions. Qualitative examples in Fig.˜1 show that CalibAtt preserves visual fidelity and temporal coherence relative to dense attention. Additional qualitative comparisons are provided in Appendix˜0.C. CalibAtt continues to show state-of-the-art results when evaluated on a distilled 4-step version of Wan 2.1 14B (Tab.˜2). Previous training-free acceleration methods may offer lower sparsity, or incur an inference-time overhead that becomes significant when applied on few-step generation, whereas CalibAtt continues to provide gains even in this setting.

4.2 Calibration Stage Ablation

Fig.˜7 analyzes the sensitivity of CalibAtt to the calibration budget and the cross-prompt agreement threshold ρ\rho. We find that the sparsity–quality tradeoff curves stabilize quickly as the number of calibration prompts increases, indicating that reliable data-independent masks can be obtained with a relatively small calibration set, further validating Observation 3. Varying ρ\rho exposes the expected tradeoff, where lower values enforce stricter cross-prompt consensus on skipped blocks and yield lower sparsity, while overly aggressive thresholds slightly degrades quality score. Based on this analysis, we use ρ=0.5\rho{=}0.5 and 6464 calibration prompts in all experiments, which provides a strong balance between sparsity and quality. Under a restricted budget, we can use 1616 prompts and reduce the Wan 2.1 14B [wan2025] 720​p720p calibration cost from 89.689.6 to 13.713.7 H100 GPU-hours with minimal effect on sparsity and quality. We elaborate on this in Sec.˜0.B.2.

5 Limitations

While CalibAtt achieves impressive sparsity and speedup numbers, our algorithm design has three key inherent limitations. First, due to CalibAtt being a strictly offline method producing data-independent masks, it may miss out on additional potential runtime gains that are specific to certain prompts or prompt types. Second, the calibration stage requires investing an initial compute budget. This one-time cost can be considered as amortized, which makes it preferable to alternative methods with inference-time overhead. Third, because it must store the calibrated masks in memory, CalibAtt increases memory usage at inference time. On Wan2.1 14B at 720​p720p, the mask memory overhead is 21.521.5GB, and it can be reduced to 3.63.6GB with minimal effect, as we show in Sec.˜0.A.2.

6 Conclusion

We present CalibAtt, a training-free method for accelerating attention in video diffusion transformers. Our approach is grounded in the observation that attention sparsity and repetition patterns, while varying across layers, heads, and timesteps, remain largely consistent across different input prompts. By performing a one-time offline calibration, our algorithm automatically identifies block-level sparsity patterns and spatially repetitive attention heads, to be leveraged for fast inference. We additionally contribute a FlashAttention3-based kernel that supports pre-computed block-sparse skip lists varying per timestep, layer, and head. Notably, CalibAtt is robust across different model architectures, output resolutions, and diffusion configurations, with no manual tuning of layer- or timestep-specific exclusions.

Several future directions related to our work remain open. First, the training-free calibration can be extended to identify attention redundancies beyond sparsity and spatial row repetition. For example, while our calibration is performed independently per timestep, exploring correlations among attention maps across consecutive timesteps may reveal additional sparsity patterns and further reduce computation. Second, the memory footprint of calibrated masks could be reduced through more compact skip list representation. Third, richer parameterizations of the energy threshold schedule ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t) and the similarity threshold γ\gamma, including layer-, head-, or timestep-dependent variants, may yield improved sparsity–quality tradeoffs. Finally, while we focus on video diffusion models, the proposed calibration framework could be generalized to other transformer-based contexts such as image diffusion and language models.

0.A.1 Hyperparameter search for energy threshold schedule

We tune the timestep-dependent energy threshold schedule ϵ​(t)\epsilon(t) in Eq.˜7 using hyperparameter optimization with Optuna [akiba2019optunanextgenerationhyperparameteroptimization]. In the high-step regime, we find that the schedule is dominated by the base level AA, while CC and kk remain relatively stable. In practice, we use

where NN is the attention sequence length for the given model and configuration. Fig.˜S8 plots the energy threshold ϵ\epsilon as a function of the normalized timestep t/Tt/T for different models and resolutions.

0.A.2 Skip-List Memory Optimization

At inference, each calibrated block mask 𝐌(t,l,h){\mathbf{M}}^{(t,l,h)} is converted into a skip-list representation that specifies, at block granularity, which attention blocks should be computed.

For a sequence of length NN and block size BB, each mask is stored as a matrix of size NB×NB+1N_{B}\times N_{B}+1. Each row corresponds to a query block-row, while the columns encode a variable-length list of contiguous intervals along the key dimension. The additional column stores the number of valid intervals for each query block-row. In the worst case, an alternating compute–skip pattern would require storing NB/2N_{B}/2 single-block intervals per row (each represented by start/end indices), forcing the skip-list matrix to retain its full width (Fig. 10(a)). Although such patterns are theoretically possible, we do not observe them in practice (Fig. S9).
Motivated by this observation, we implement skip-list matrices with mask-dependent column lengths in our kernel. Concretely, instead of allocating a fixed-width matrix of size NBN_{B} columns for every mask, we trim each skip-list matrix to the maximum interval index populated by any query block-row (Fig. 10(b)), preserving a dense layout shared across rows while significantly reducing padding. For Wan-T2V 14B at 720​p720p, the full set of untrimmed skip-list matrices totals 5252 GB. Per-layer trimming alone reduces this to 21.521.5 GB with no effect on correctness or sparsity which we use for the 720​p720p results reported in Tab. 1.
Tab. S3 further reports the effect of progressively merging nearby intervals, which intentionally marks a small number of additional blocks for computation in exchange for reduced padding (Fig. 10(c)). In particular, interval merging reduces the footprint to 6.36.3 GB with neglible sparsity loss.
Finally, a more compact alternative stores all intervals in a flat one-dimensional array and maintains a per-row offset and length for each query block-row, eliminating padding entirely. Tab. S3 shows that this representation alone reduces the footprint to 4.04.0 GB without any merging. The same interval merging strategy applies, yielding identical merged intervals for each percentile. We leave the efficient kernel implementation of the 1D representation to future work and report only VBench scores and sparsity levels.

0.A.3 Timestep Mask Sharing

We observe that calibrated masks become increasingly similar at later timesteps. To quantify this, we compute pairwise Intersection over Union (IoU):

IoU​(t1,t2)=|S(t1)∩S(t2)||S(t1)∪S(t2)|,\mathrm{IoU}(t_{1},t_{2})\;=\;\frac{\left|S^{(t_{1})}\cap S^{(t_{2})}\right|}{\left|S^{(t_{1})}\cup S^{(t_{2})}\right|}, (S12)

where S(t)S^{(t)} denotes the set of skipped blocks at timestep tt, averaged over layers and heads. As shown in Fig.˜S11, later timesteps exhibit high pairwise IoU. This suggests that masks can be shared across timesteps, reducing memory footprint without affecting inference. For each (layer, head) pair, we greedily cluster timesteps into cliques: a timestep joins a cluster only if its IoU with all existing cluster members exceeds a threshold τ\tau. All timesteps in a cluster share a single mask, obtained by taking the logical OR over the kept-block masks of all cluster members. Combined with the skip-list optimizations described in Sec.˜0.A.2, timestep sharing further reduces the inference-time footprint to 3.63.6GB for 2D skip lists and 2.22.2GB for 1D skip lists. Tab.˜S3 reports results for τ∈{0.97,0.98}\tau\in\{0.97,0.98\}, showing the trade-off between memory reduction and sparsity loss.

0.B.1 Spatial Repetition Anchor Rows

0.B.2 Calibration Stage Compute Cost

The calibration stage consists of two main components: computing block energies for mask calibration (Sec.˜3.2), for which we implemented a custom CUDA kernel, and computing spatial similarity scores for repetition detection (Sec.˜3.3), which uses an optimized PyTorch implementation. Both computations run over a set of calibration prompts, but can use different numbers of prompts independently. As shown in Fig.˜6(b), the standard deviation of spatial similarity scores across prompts is low for high-similarity maps, suggesting that fewer prompts suffice for reliable repetition detection. Similarly, Fig.˜7 shows that for our selected threshold ρ=0.5\rho=0.5, quality remains stable across different calibration set sizes beyond a minimal number of prompts, motivating the use of fewer prompts for mask calibration as well. We ablate on different numbers of prompts for each component in Tab.˜S5 for Wan 2.1 14B at 720​p720p with 5050 timesteps, reporting the total calibration cost in H100 GPU-hours. Since similarity computation has lower cross-prompt variance, we can use as few as 1 prompt for similarity while using more prompts for mask calibration. This allows trading off calibration time against quality, enabling faster calibration under restricted compute budgets. In particular, using 16 prompts for mask calibration and just 1 prompt for similarity computation reduces calibration time to 13.7 GPU-hours while maintaining quality and semantic scores comparable to the dense FA3 baseline. The calibration cost could be reduced further through a more efficient implementation of the similarity computation, or by fusing the similarity and block energy computations into a single kernel. We leave these optimizations for future work.

0.B.3 Kernel Block Size

FlashAttention [dao2022flashattention] partitions the attention computation into blocks of size Bq×Bk​vB_{q}\times B_{kv}, where the optimal sizes depend on the head dimension dd and available shared memory (SHMEM) on the GPU. For a given head dimension, larger block sizes improve hardware utilization up to the SHMEM limit. For d=128d=128 as in Wan [wan2025], FlashAttention uses 128×176128\times 176 as its native configuration on H100 GPUs. Our energy-based block selection in Eq.˜6 is largely invariant to block size since the energy threshold ϵ\epsilon specifies the fraction of attention mass to retain and the same spatial regions are selected regardless of how they are partitioned into blocks. However, larger block sizes can reduce achievable sparsity. When a block is selected, all its entries are computed, even if only a subset contributes significant attention mass. Thus, coarser blocks may include more redundant computation, or fewer blocks may be marked as skippable. We illustrate this effect in Fig.˜S14. This reveals a tradeoff where larger blocks are faster per block but yield lower sparsity, while smaller blocks achieve higher sparsity but with reduced per-block efficiency. We evaluate this in Tab.˜S6 on the VBench evaluation suite using block sizes supported by FlashAttention, using the same calibrated ϵ\epsilon schedule throughout and reporting mask sparsity only (excluding spatial repetition from 3.3) to isolate the effect, with dense FA3 included as reference. As expected, smaller blocks yield higher sparsity. However, the improved hardware utilization of larger blocks compensates for the reduced sparsity, and these two effects balance out across a wide range of configurations. From 128×96128\times 96 onward, all block sizes achieve comparable speedups of 1.411.41–1.42×1.42\times while maintaining comparable VBench scores, demonstrating that our method is robust to block size choice and compatible with the native FA3 configuration.
